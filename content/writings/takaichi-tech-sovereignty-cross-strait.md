Japan's new Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has been portrayed as a staunch nationalist and the ideological heir of former prime minister Shinzo Abe — pro-Taiwan, anti-China and hawkish on defence. Yet her first few weeks in office tell a different story. Rather than symbolic confrontation, Takaichi has placed her political capital on crisis management investment and digital deterrence — doctrines that fuse economic security with technological sovereignty. Her agenda marks a shift from ideological posturing to infrastructural power, positioning technology as the foundation of Japan's national strategy and regional diplomacy.

Takaichi's key departure from her predecessors lies in how she redefines Japan's digital agenda. Where earlier leaders saw digitalisation as administrative reform, Takaichi treats it as national defence. Her flagship concept of "crisis management investment" merges economic and military logic — using fiscal expansion in artificial intelligence, semiconductors and quantum computing to secure what she calls Japan's "growth through security."

This approach folds digital and industrial policy into Japan's strategic posture. The latest National Security Strategy now lists cyber and cognitive warfare alongside traditional threats, marking a shift from digital transformation to digital deterrence. Takaichi has been explicit that "wars now begin in the digital domain," framing data protection and supply-chain resilience as acts of sovereignty rather than efficiency.

This evolution carries both opportunity and heightened risk for Taiwan, which now stands at the crossroads of China's digital and industrial offensive. In 2025, Taiwanese authorities reported a surge in cyberattacks originating from China, aimed at undermining public trust and state resilience. Around the same time, suspects were detained for stealing TSMC's chip-making secrets, underscoring how semiconductors — the backbone of Taiwan's economy — have become the primary target of industrial espionage and strategic coercion. For Taiwan, advanced chips, AI systems and resilient digital infrastructure are not only commercial assets but also the instruments of deterrence and diplomacy.

Takaichi's doctrine of crisis management investment mirrors Taiwan's logic: technology as sovereignty and deterrence. Cooperation could extend well beyond production to joint cybersecurity frameworks, shared data standards and coordinated export controls aligned with U.S. strategic priorities. Taiwan's semiconductor dominance and frontline cyber-defence experience make it a crucial contributor, while Japan provides fiscal and industrial capacity. Embedded within the U.S.–Japan alliance and the Free and Open Indo-Pacific strategy, this partnership can anchor a trusted technology network linking Taiwan, Japan, India and Australia. Together, they would secure semiconductor and AI supply chains from coercion and form a core pillar of Indo-Pacific economic security that reinforces, rather than duplicates, Washington's regional strategy.

Yet this new phase of collaboration may also signal a drift toward managed competition. Japan's ambition for technological self-sufficiency — reinforced by tighter scrutiny of foreign tech partnerships — could gradually reduce its reliance on Taiwan's manufacturing ecosystem. As Tokyo redefines digital infrastructure as a matter of national security, it may privilege domestic production and restrict sensitive technology flows. What was once a complementary industrial relationship risks evolving into parallel supply chains divided by security boundaries. For Taiwan, Japan's technological nationalism is both a strategic shield and an emerging constraint.

China, meanwhile, faces a deeper challenge than rhetoric. While Beijing has long framed Takaichi as a hard-line conservative, her policies threaten to erode China's structural influence rather than provoke symbolic confrontation. Her decision to delay visiting the Yasukuni Shrine was less a gesture of restraint than a calculation to maintain diplomatic manoeuvrability ahead of potential talks with President Xi Jinping. Behind that pragmatism lies a clear objective — to cut China's dominance over the technologies and materials underpinning regional power.

Tokyo's new focus on rare-earth cooperation with the United States and Pacific partners directly targets one of China's strongest tools of economic coercion. By securing alternative sources of critical minerals and embedding these efforts within the U.S.–Japan alliance, Japan reduces its vulnerability to Chinese export control and strengthens the supply-chain architecture linking Taiwan, India and Australia. For Beijing, this signals a longer-term shift: Japan's rearmament is no longer military in form but technological and resource-driven, aiming to redefine the foundations of Asian interdependence itself.

By weaving technology, supply-chain security and resource policy into a single strategic framework, Japan is reshaping the structural environment that defines cross-strait relations. Its pursuit of digital deterrence, rare-earth independence and regional technology standards is reducing Asia's systemic dependence on China while creating new layers of interdependence around Japan. This emerging order complicates both Taiwan's leverage and China's influence.

For Taiwan, the challenge will be to turn alignment into adaptability — investing in cybersecurity, cross-border data governance and next-generation chip innovation that sustain its strategic value as networks regionalise. For China, recalibrating engagement through stable economic channels and transparent technology norms could mitigate isolation and prevent digital confrontation from hardening into economic bloc competition.

Commentators have called Takaichi the second Abe, but the comparison understates the scope of her transformation. Abe sought constitutional reform to normalise Japan's military posture; Takaichi seeks technological sovereignty to secure the country's economic and strategic autonomy. Takaichi's Japan sits at the centre of this transformation. Her drive for technological sovereignty has turned infrastructure into the new measure of influence — and that shift will quietly but decisively redefine the balance across the Taiwan Strait.

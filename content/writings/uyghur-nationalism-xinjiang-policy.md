The ongoing conflict in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region (XUAR) represents a profound crisis of ethnic nationalism and statehood in Western China. The evolution of Uyghur ethnic nationalism cannot be decoupled from its historical interaction with Chinese state policy. The persistent cycle of conflict, marked by accumulating grievances and mutual suspicion between the Han majority and the Uyghur minority, is the direct result of a series of misaligned Xinjiang policies. To understand this trajectory, one must examine the iterative transition of Beijing's approach, from initial economic inducement to securitized repression, and the subsequent mobilization of a more complex, politicized ethnic consciousness among the Uyghurs.

## 1. Origins of Uyghur Ethnic Nationalism

The integration of Xinjiang into the People's Republic of China (PRC) in 1955, following the dissolution of the Second East Turkestan Republic, established the foundation for a volatile administrative relationship. Ruling the XUAR proved difficult for the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) due to distinctive internal factors that actively cultivated ethnic nationalism.

These internal dynamics were rooted in deep-seated cultural heterogeneity, characterized by distinct Islamic religious traditions and linguistic differences that fostered a collective identity among Uyghurs, separating them from the Han population.<sup>1</sup> These sociocultural distinctions gained renewed political salience due to the state's increased, albeit inconsistent, tolerance for the revival of Islamic institutions and Uyghur cultural practices following the Cultural Revolution.<sup>2</sup> The expansion of religious infrastructure was significant; while there were only 392 mosques across the region in the 1970s, this number surged to 4,700 by 1981.<sup>3</sup> The concurrent proliferation of Islamic schools throughout the 1980s further solidified this communal identity.<sup>4</sup>

For the Chinese state, these developments were not merely cultural; they were perceived as existential. The revitalization of Islamic culture was increasingly framed as "an ideology competing with Marxism," posing a direct challenge to the CCP's ideological hegemony and regional stability.<sup>5</sup> Simultaneously, the region suffered from economic isolation. Unlike China's coastal provinces, Xinjiang failed to benefit from the market-oriented economic reforms of the era, remaining trapped in an agricultural paradigm with minimal industrialization, effectively cut off from the national economic ascent.<sup>6</sup>

In 1981, paramount leader Deng Xiaoping visited the region to formulate a corrective strategy. His solution relied on two pillars: the institutional expansion of the Xinjiang Production and Construction Corps (XPCC) and a program of "inducement policies." The XPCC, an economic and paramilitary institution predominantly staffed by Han Chinese, was deployed to exploit natural resources and stimulate economic growth. Concurrently, the state introduced fiscal and social incentives to minimize regional disparities, including fixed fiscal subsidies that saw an annual increase of 10 percent from 1980 to 1989.<sup>7</sup> Furthermore, affirmative action programs in university entrance examinations were designed to facilitate social mobilization.<sup>8</sup>

These policies, however, failed to achieve the intended stabilization. Instead, ethnic nationalism intensified throughout the 1980s. Student protests in Urumqi in 1985 demanding the removal of Han migrants and advocating for "Independence, Freedom, and Sovereignty," followed by the 1989 protests over official religious disrespect, signaled the failure of Deng's economic-centric approach.<sup>9</sup> While regional CCP Secretary Wang Enmao correctly identified "ethnic separatism" as the primary threat, this "ethnic threat theory" was not yet central to Deng's national strategy, leaving a complex, unresolved legacy for his successor, Jiang Zemin.<sup>10</sup>

## 2. The 1990s Riots and the Transition of Policies

The 1990s marked a departure from Deng's reliance on economic integration toward a more securitized framework. The Baren Township incident in 1990 served as a critical inflection point, reflecting widespread Uyghur dissatisfaction with PRC birth control policies, nuclear testing in the region, and resource exploitation. The incident's explicit call for "a jihad to drive Han unbelievers out of Xinjiang and establish an East Turkestan" underscored the radicalization of the nationalist movement.<sup>11</sup>

Following this, political violence escalated between 1990 and 1996, with bombing incidents occurring in Yining, Urumqi, and Kashgar, alongside targeted assassinations of state officials.<sup>12</sup> This climate of unrest compelled the CCP to adopt a more rigid stance. In 1996, the party issued the "Record of the Meeting of the Standing Committee of the Political Bureau of the Chinese Communist Party concerning the maintenance of Stability in Xinjiang," known as Document #7. This document officially codified "national separatism and illegal religious activity" as the primary threats to regional stability, explicitly calling for the implementation of comprehensive, repressive ethnic and religious policies alongside economic measures.<sup>13</sup>

Document #7 signified a pivotal shift in the CCP's interpretive framework: conflicts were now officially categorized as ethnic in nature, requiring state intervention beyond mere development. However, the subsequent Yining Incident in 1997 illustrated the counterproductive nature of this repression. Triggered by government restrictions on traditional social gatherings, the ensuing protest resulted in over 300 deaths and the disappearance of 8,000 individuals, according to exile sources.<sup>14</sup> Empirical observation suggests that the frequency and severity of riots actually increased post-1996, indicating that the repression-heavy approach of Document #7 exacerbated, rather than mitigated, ethnic polarization.<sup>15</sup> The government's interference in cultural and social life served to harden Uyghur perception of the Han as "invaders," transforming a struggle for autonomy into a more visceral, hate-driven resistance.

## 3. Western Development: Asymmetric Integration

In 2000, Jiang Zemin initiated the "Western Development" strategy, distinguishing his administration from Deng's by leveraging the XPCC to exert comprehensive social, cultural, and economic control. Under this strategy, the XPCC's authority expanded into local legal systems, government administration, and economic management, effectively enhancing its capacity to oversee industries and local populations.<sup>16</sup> The resulting influx of Han migration, which saw the XPCC population reach 2.4 million, further marginalized the Uyghur population.<sup>17</sup>

While the period between 2000 and 2009 appeared relatively peaceful, this stability was superficial.<sup>18</sup> Beneath it, structural inequalities fermented. Urumqi's demographic landscape, where Han Chinese constituted the vast majority of the 1.4 million residents while Uyghurs were concentrated in impoverished prefectures like Kashgar, highlighted a glaring economic disparity.<sup>19</sup> Exclusionary hiring policies in state-run oil and cotton industries further cemented Uyghur grievances, as the benefits of regional resource extraction accrued largely to the state and Han-dominated institutions rather than the local population.<sup>20, 21, 22</sup>

Simultaneously, modernization projects and Han migration precipitated intense cultural conflict. The destruction of traditional urban landscapes to make way for Western-style, Han-centric architecture was perceived by Uyghurs as an encroachment upon their heritage.<sup>23</sup> This systemic marginalization reinforced the opposition between the two ethnic groups, setting the stage for the 2009 Urumqi riots.

## 4. The 2009 Urumqi Riot and the "Three Evils"

The 2009 Urumqi riot, which originated from a brawl in a Guangdong toy factory, escalated into a violent, widespread ethnic conflict that resulted in 184 deaths, with Han casualties numbering 134. This event exposed the depth of mutual mistrust that had accumulated under years of state management.

In response, Hu Jintao's administration codified a new discursive strategy: the "Three Evils" — terrorism, separatism, and religious extremism. This framework, which emerged during the formation of the Shanghai Cooperation Organization (SCO), became the official lens through which all Xinjiang-related instability was interpreted.<sup>24</sup> The "Three Evils" rhetoric represented a final transition in the CCP's policy evolution: moving from "ethnic threat theory" to "economic development" and, ultimately, to a securitized approach centered on counter-terrorism.

Hu's rhetoric explicitly prioritized stability as the "most important mission," advocating for intensified military and police control over the economic development strategies favored by his predecessors. By framing the conflict purely as a response to terrorism and criminal activity, the state conveniently ignored the underlying socioeconomic grievances and cultural alienation that fueled the unrest. This policy of simplification arguably made the conflict more intractable, as the state treated the symptoms of ethnic grievance while ignoring the structural causes.

## 5. Conclusion

The trajectory of Uyghur nationalism reflects a transition from primordial identity consciousness to a complex, politicized ideology. Originally predicated on linguistic and religious difference, this nationalism was systematically reconstructed through state policies that emphasized economic disparity and cultural marginalization. Policies such as the "Western Development" plan, while intended to integrate the region, inadvertently provided the conditions for this comprehensive nationalism to expand.

The Chinese government has cycled through various theoretical frameworks to manage Xinjiang, from inducement and ethnic threat theories to the current securitized counter-terrorism model. However, each iteration has failed to rectify the problematic economic structure or resolve deepening cultural conflicts. By consistently misinterpreting the root causes of the unrest and maintaining a policy of ignorance toward the sociopolitical reality of the region, the Chinese state has deepened the cycle of conflict. The brutal crackdown, while stabilizing the region through force, has arguably only heightened the stakes for all involved.

Ultimately, rectifying these conflicts requires a move away from the authoritarian reflex of securitization toward inclusive political engagement. While international pressure, exemplified by the increased scrutiny of re-education facilities, has forced limited transparency, the internal resolution of this crisis depends on the capacity for Uyghurs to organize civil society and articulate their grievances through stable political pathways. Without addressing the foundational issues of inequality and cultural recognition, the future of Xinjiang remains precariously poised between continued state-led suppression and the escalation of an entrenched, nationalist resistance.
